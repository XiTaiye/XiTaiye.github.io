Overcoming Your Fear of Competition

Would you rather apply to a job opportunity that 10 people have applied to or an essentially identical opportunity that has 100 applicants?

If we guessed correctly, you’ve probably answered “the first one.”

Studies across research disciplines (including decision-making, social psychology, management, and consumer psychology) have all found that people are often demotivated when facing a large number of competitors, but are fiercely competitive when they feel that they are competing with just a few rivals.

To illustrate, consider a study on the TV game show, The Weakest Link. In this competition, players were asked to vote to eliminate other players in order to increase their own chance of winning the grand prize. As the number of players remaining in the game decreased, players became more competitive — they were more likely to target and vote out top performers. Another study on competitive behavior in auction settings showed that when there were fewer participants, bidders were not only more aggressive, they were also more likely to overspend just to outbid their rivals.

On the other hand, when faced with a larger number of competitors, research shows that people feel a sense of dread — even when such fear is objectively irrational and can have negative consequences. For instance, a landmark study examining the psychology of competition found that students taking the SAT at venues with more people performed worse on the exam than those at venues with fewer test takers.

Across academic, professional and all other life domains, you may frequently face competitive situations involving large groups of people. So how can you overcome your innate dread, bolster your performance motivation and, of course, become a winner? Below, we discuss the psychological factors that can influence your competitive drive and can be leveraged to help you succeed.

1) Understand the psychology behind competition.

The degraded motivation we feel when competing against larger groups of people is driven by multiple psychological factors. For instance, research indicates that as the number of competitors increases, it becomes increasingly more difficult to compare one’s own progress to that of other individuals, which can be frustrating and demotivating.

People can also be irrationally demotivated by competition size because of the imperfect nature of their subjective perceptions. Our own research shows that even when social comparison doesn’t play a role, larger-sized competitions alter people’s perceived likelihood of winning, as well as their perception of the size of the prize. Take one of the studies we conducted as an example: The competition scenario required participants to stay physically active for four weeks, and the top 10% performers would each win a $75 cash prize. Participants were much less motivated to compete when there were 5,000 participants versus 50 participants. Despite the objective winning likelihood (10%) and cash prize ($75) being identical in both situations, people subjectively perceived the wining likelihood as much lower and the prize as smaller when there were 5,000 (vs. 50) participants. These imperfect subjective perceptions in turn negatively impacted their competitive drive.

2) Harness the power of social comparison.

One way to overcome the psychological barriers mentioned above is to proactively compare yourself to specific competitors. Doing so thoughtfully and with the intention of self-improvement can help you excel. When information about someone else’s performance is salient in your mind, you’ll be more driven to perform as well as or better than them, and equally importantly, not look bad in front of them.

For instance, imagine that you just joined a major firm and that the top echelon of new employees this year will be recommended for promotion. You can activate the power of social comparison by consciously paying attention to the performance of your top colleagues. The key here is to use comparison to progress instead of merely feeling envious. You should consciously compare how they are doing things differently from you and think about how you can do better. Let these purposeful comparisons and the resulting feeling of wanting to be as great/superior drive you to perform better.

3) Reframe the likelihood of winning.

Another way to motivate yourself is to reframe your mindset. How you think about a competition can greatly influence the actions you take during it. For instance, your level of motivation depends on how likely you believe you are to succeed. The same likelihood of winning can feel more appealing when you represent the information about the competition in your mind differently. For instance, let’s say you’re competing in a national-level exam, and of the 200,000 exam takers, only the top 10% will receive an A rank. This information will tend be subjectively less intimidating if you mentally reframe it as, “on average, 1 in 10 exam takers will receive an A rank.”

As another example, imagine that you are applying to a job at a leading global firm during a career fair. Their handout says, “Of the 800 job applicants this season, only the top 20% will be offered an in-person interview.” Instead of letting the numbers intimidate you, reframe the likelihood as, “On average, 1 in 5 applicants will get an in-person interview.” That feels more motivating, right?

4) Reframe the prize.

Your perception of the competition prize can also be enhanced to evoke a stronger competitive drive. Take a competition with 10,000 participants and 10% chance of winning a $100 prize, as an example. Now, imagine that instead of thinking about “a $100 prize,” you mentally reframe the prize as “winning a share of a $100,000 prize.” By doing this, the reward can feel psychologically more substantial and hence more motivating.

You can also use this approach (as well as the aforementioned two approaches) to motivate others to compete. For instance, imagine that you are working for a major chocolate brand and that you’ve been tasked with organizing a viral video competition to help promote the brand on social media. Of the 50,000 video postings by consumers, the brand will reward the top 30%, ranked by the number of video views, with a 1-pound chocolate bar. Instead of “top posters will each get a 1-pound chocolate bar,” reframing the prize information as “top posters will each get a share of 7.5 tons of chocolate” can help make the reward feel much larger (and more fun) to your customers.

Although you may innately dread facing larger numbers of competitors, such aversion can be overcome when you mentally reframe your construal of the competition. Leveraging psychological forces such as social comparison, enhanced perceptions of success likelihood and competition prize, you can bolster your drive to jump into the race and start winning.
