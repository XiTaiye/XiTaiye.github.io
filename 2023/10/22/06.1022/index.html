PCM can help you communicate with every personality type

Efficient communication is essential for a business, it serves as the cornerstone of productivity and collaboration. In my case, communication plays an even bigger role. I am a communication expert and communication is the foundation of my business. Also, I need to be in contact with different kinds of people every day—my clients, colleagues, and partners. Often, I adjust my communication strategy to the person I’m dealing with at the moment.

All people are different. For instance, some prefer logical explanations over emotional ones, while others make choices based primarily on how they feel. For a long time I’ve been exploring the diversity of communication styles through the lens of psychology. I tried all the major personality assessment tests: MBTI, Hogan, DISC, Big Five, Enneagram, 16PF, and career aptitude tests. Exploring each of these typologies has improved my understanding of others and of self, making me a better communicator.

The process communication model (PCM) is one of the personality assessment methods that I have found most helpful. PCM has a solid foundation in psychology and is flexible enough to not oversimplify human nature.

What is PCM?

PCM is a communication model formulated by Dr. Taibi Kahler, a renowned psychologist and recipient of the Eric Berne Memorial Scientific Award who has also worked with NASA. Dr. Kahler developed PCM in the 1970s, drawing inspiration from observing and analyzing communication patterns of successful communicators, such as politicians and motivational speakers. Today, PCM is widely recognized and used by Pixar, Microsoft, Ikea, Coca-Cola, and other well-known companies.

PCM provides a framework to understand and decode individual personality features, enabling us to tailor our communication to each person’s unique needs. By recognizing and adapting to different communication styles, PCM offers a powerful tool for building stronger relationships and achieving better outcomes from teamwork and collaboration.

Understanding communication styles

At its core, PCM classifies individuals into six personality types based on the preferential “filter” used by a person to perceive reality: emotions, thoughts, opinions, actions, reactions, and reflections. These personality types are: Thinker, Persister, Harmonizer, Imaginer, Rebel, and Promoter. They are not meant to put people in boxes, but rather to provide a common language for understanding and engaging with others.

PCM teaches that everyone has a preferred way of communicating and receiving information, and that by recognizing these preferences, we can adjust our interactions to maximize understanding and minimize conflicts. By aligning our communication style with the preferences of others, we can establish rapport, build trust, and, consequently, foster more productive conversations.

Moreover, PCM helps us recognize when others express stress reactions and allows us to adapt how we communicate accordingly. Each personality type responds to stress differently. For instance, some individuals require space and others seek support. Understanding these differences can help you communicate more effectively.

Practical applications of PCM

Let’s go through a couple of examples to better understand how PCM can be applied to our personal and professional lives. For instance, you work on a project with a client who is a Persister. They are meticulous and detail-oriented and need reassurance. It may conflict with the communication style of your personality type.

Let’s say you are a Promoter and, in contrast with a Persister, you are more focused on the big picture and tend to overlook the need for reassurance. To adapt to a Persister’s communication style, provide them with regular updates and detailed progress reports throughout a given project. If they have any concerns, address them promptly and provide reassurance.

Here is another example. Let’s say your colleague is an Imaginer, they are creative and focused on possibilities, while you are a Thinker and prefer structure and logical reasoning. In this dynamic the Imaginer should strive to present their ideas with more structure and provide logical reasoning to support them. The Thinker, on the other hand, can initiate creative brainstorming sessions to help the Imaginer to show their best qualities. The effort from both sides can help achieve a balance between creativity and structure.

How to start using PCM

To figure out the personality types of people with whom you are interacting, you need to listen to the way a person speaks. By carefully listening, you can identify what is the main “filter” of that person and align your communication with it.

Here is an exercise to practice implementing PCM in your communication: Imagine that you have a business meeting with all the PCM personalities at the table. Take the part of each of these characters one at a time, using their words and needs intrinsic to them. It’ll help you understand the strong points and weak spots of your communication.

There are also helpful resources about the PCM personalities online as well as numerous PCM workshops you can attend. Keep in mind that all PCM trainers and coaches should be certified by Kahler Communications, the company founded by Dr. Kahler.

How leaders can use PCM

PCM has significant implications for leadership and team dynamics. By employing PCM, managers can adapt their communication styles to motivate and engage team members effectively. Understanding the personalities within your team enables leaders to assign tasks, provide feedback, and resolve conflicts in ways that resonate with each individual, thereby boosting productivity and team synergy. In a world where miscommunication and misunderstandings are still too common, PCM can be a key to unlocking the full potential of professional and personal relationships. PCM can help you to develop systematic communication skills that allow you to nurture meaningful connections and achieve better outcomes from collaborations.
